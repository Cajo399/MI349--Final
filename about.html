  • Virtual Reality MECH Study

    This project examines the technical and theoretical development of a virtual reality environment for a study of human-avatar embodiment.

    Student researchers developed many new skills through this project. Specifically, our team used the platform Unity to create the experimental stimuli, and the programming language C# within Unity to develop the environment.

    The study involves a virtual reality environment wherein virtual agents ("MECHs") host participants as they volunteer for a simulated disaster relief task.

    Further, we used the digital painting software Krita to assign differentiating colors and patterns to the agent's virtual 'bodies.' These design differences are then assigned to participants themselves to further their identification with the virtual hosts during the simulation. Likewise, we designed the agents to speak to the participants throughout the experiment to reiterate such stimuli and other attitudes we programmed them to express.

    Text-to-speech software was used to generate these audio files and we finalized them with 'Audacity' to splice them into manageable chunks. Once the files were properly formatted, they were implemented into the virtual environment.

    Overall, our development team utilized software such as Unity, Krita, and Audacity to perfect the virtual experience for participants. Through this process, we learned many valuable development skills to enhance our research abilities and facilitate this study of human-agent interaction.

  • Improving Advising at MSU (MI 451)

    This study explores ways to enhance the student advising experience at Michigan State University.

    Through eight contextual interviews with students from various departments, we identified both positive and negative aspects of current advising practices.

    Positive experiences highlighted the importance of clear communication and personalized support, while negative experiences underscored issues such as lack of engagement and inconsistent information.

    Based on these findings, we brainstormed and prototyped several design solutions, including an advising chatbot, automated scheduling system, advising tablet rooms, and telephone booth advising.

    Our final prototype, the advising tablet rooms with a complementary mobile application, aims to provide accessible and reliable advising information.

    Evaluation of this prototype revealed areas for improvement, such as button clarity and information organization. Future work will focus on refining the design and conducting further evaluations to enhance the advising experience for MSU students.

    My Role in This Project

    The team was assigned to devise a way to improve the student advising experience at Michigan State University.

    I was responsible for conducting contextual interviews with MSU students, analyzing the user data, and creating storyboards for the design concepts.

    I interviewed 2 students from various colleges and asked them about their experiences, needs, and expectations with advisors. I then synthesized these findings and identified the positive and negative aspects of advising at MSU. I also created two storyboards to illustrate how the telephone booth advising and the advising tablet room concepts would work in different scenarios.

    As a result, I was able to contribute to the research and design process by providing insights into the student perspective and creating prototypes that addressed the main problems and goals of the users. I also collaborated with my teammates to refine the design solutions and to create the final report.
